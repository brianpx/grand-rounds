Multimodal Analgesia in Spine Surgery

A Comprehensive Study Guide and Interactive Learning Resource

Based on Grand Rounds Presentation (October 14, 2026) by Deepthi Penta, M.D.

Assistant Professor, General Anesthesiology

Created by Brian Pitts, M.D., M.S., MEHP

Overview

  • Definition: MMA = synergistic use of 2+ analgesic agents with different mechanisms
  • Context: >1.2 million spine surgeries annually in US (5-75% high risk for severe postoperative pain)
  • Problem: 20-70% of patients present with preoperative opioid use → poorer outcomes
  • Major Finding: "Maximal" MMA (3+ agents) provides additional benefits over standard care

1. The Rationale for MMA in Spine Surgery

A. The Challenge of Postoperative Pain

Consequences of Inadequate Pain Management:

  • Decreased mobility
  • Increased complication rates (pneumonia, DVT)
  • Negative impacts on social/emotional well-being

Benefits of Adequate Pain Control:

  • Superior mobility and coordination
  • Lower complication risk
  • Quicker recovery
  • Better surgical outcomes
  • Improved patient satisfaction

B. Limitations of Traditional Opioid-Based Analgesia

  • Delayed wound healing
  • Urinary retention
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Development of tolerance

C. Understanding Pain Pathways

Peripheral Pathway (Primary Hyperalgesia)

  • Surgical incision → cellular damage/inflammation
  • Activates prostaglandins (PGE-2, IL-6)
  • Overstimulation of peripheral nociceptors → acute pain

Central Pathway (Secondary Hyperalgesia)

  • NMDA receptor activation → prolonged CNS stimulation
  • Central sensitization → chronic pain
  • Mediators: COX, nitric oxide synthase (NOS)

2. Impact of Preoperative Opioid Use

A. Prevalence and Negative Outcomes

Prevalence: 20-70% use opioids preoperatively (nearly 20% opioid-dependent)

Associated Poor Outcomes:

  • Higher inpatient opioid consumption
  • Higher postoperative pain scores
  • Greater post-surgical disability
  • Increased hospital LOS
  • Worsened functional/surgical outcomes
  • Increased 90-day complications

B. Preoperative Opioid Tapering

  • Total Joint Arthroplasty Study: 50% reduction in morphine-equivalent dose → significantly improved functional outcomes
  • Spine Surgery Review: Patients on MEA >47.8 mg/day less likely to achieve minimum clinically important difference

C. Opioid-Induced Hyperalgesia (OIH)

  • Definition: Paradoxical increased pain sensitivity from chronic opioid exposure
  • Mechanism: NMDA receptor activation
  • Consequences: Loss of opioid efficacy, treatment challenges

3. Analgesic Agents and Modalities

Methadone

  • Potent mu-receptor agonist with long half-life (24-36 hours)
  • NMDA receptor antagonist + serotonin/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor
  • Dosing: 0.15-0.2 mg/kg ideal body weight (max 20 mg)
  • Clinical evidence shows decreased pain/opioids for 72 hours vs fentanyl

NSAIDs

  • COX enzyme inhibition → blocked prostaglandin production
  • Concern: Risk of nonunion is dose/duration-dependent
  • High-dose ketorolac (>120 mg/day) increases risk
  • Parecoxib → 39% reduction in morphine use

Gabapentinoids

  • Inhibit alpha 2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels
  • Reduce excitatory neurotransmitter release
  • Caution: 50% increased odds of naloxone requirement with opioids

Ketamine

  • CNS NMDA receptor antagonist
  • ASRA recommended for opioid-tolerant patients
  • Dosing: 0.35 mg/kg bolus + 0.1-0.5 mg/kg/hr infusion
  • Meta-analysis: Lower pain and opioid use up to 48 hours

4. "Maximal" MMA Efficacy

Meta-Analysis Key Findings (12 studies)

≥3 agents vs ≤2 agents:

  • Significantly decreased pain scores at 12, 24, and 48 hours
  • Decreased opioid consumption at 24 and 48 hours
  • Significantly shorter hospital LOS
  • No significant difference in PONV or respiratory events

5. Regional Anesthesia: ESP Block

  • Paraspinal fascial plane block between erector spinae and transverse process
  • Blocks dorsal rami, potential paravertebral spread
  • 8/9 studies: significant opioid reduction (up to 48 hours)
  • 9/9 studies: significant pain score decrease

6. Conclusion

  • MMA should be standard of care for spine surgery pain management
  • "Maximal" approach (3+ agents) yields superior outcomes
  • Future research needed on optimal combinations and personalized approaches

Study Guide

Short-Answer Questions

1. What is the definition of multimodal analgesia (MMA), and what is its primary goal?

Multimodal analgesia is defined as the simultaneous use of two or more agents that act synergistically through different mechanisms to provide pain control. Its goal is to improve pain control by targeting different pathways, thereby reducing the amount of opioids needed and their associated adverse side effects.

2. Describe the negative effects of preoperative opioid use on outcomes for spine surgery patients.

Preoperative opioid use is associated with several negative outcomes, including higher inpatient opioid consumption, higher pain scores, and greater post-operative disability. It can also lead to increased hospital length of stay, worsened functional/surgical outcomes, and an increased likelihood of 90-day complications.

3. Explain the phenomenon of Opioid-Induced Hyperalgesia (OIH) and identify the receptor that may play a central role in its development.

Opioid-Induced Hyperalgesia is a paradoxical adverse response defined as an increased sensitivity to painful stimuli resulting from chronic opioid exposure. NMDA receptor activation is thought to play a central role in this phenomenon, which can lead to a loss of opioid efficacy.

4. Methadone has multi-receptor pharmacology. Besides being a potent mu-receptor agonist, what are its other mechanisms of action?

In addition to being an opioid mu-receptor agonist, methadone is also a potent NMDA receptor antagonist and a serotonin/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor. These properties may help counteract opioid-induced hyperalgesia.

5. What are the primary concerns associated with the use of NSAIDs in spinal fusion surgeries, and how might these concerns be mitigated?

The primary concerns are pseudoarthrosis, nonunion, and post-operative bleeding. A meta-analysis suggests the risk of nonunion is associated with high-dose ketorolac (>120 mg/day), but not with short-term use of NSAIDs at normal doses, and animal studies indicate the risk may be mitigated with selective COX-2 inhibitors like celecoxib.

Glossary

Acetaminophen

A central inhibitor of COX enzymes (COX-1, -2, -3) and prostaglandin synthesis. Front-line analgesic for mild to moderate pain.

Central Sensitization

Prolonged CNS stimulation via NMDA receptor activation, leading to secondary hyperalgesia and chronic pain.

COX Enzymes

Cyclooxygenase enzymes that mediate prostaglandin synthesis. COX-1 affects GI mucosa/platelets; COX-2 is specific to inflammatory tissues.

ESP Block

Erector Spinae Plane Block - paraspinal fascial plane block between erector spinae muscle and thoracic transverse process.

Gabapentinoids

Drugs (gabapentin, pregabalin) that inhibit alpha 2-delta subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels on presynaptic axons.

Ketamine

NMDA receptor antagonist that decreases nociceptive signal sensitization. Also decreases serotonin/norepinephrine reuptake.

Methadone

Potent opioid mu-receptor agonist with long half-life, NMDA antagonist, and serotonin/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor.

MMA

Multimodal Analgesia - simultaneous use of 2+ analgesic agents with different mechanisms for synergistic pain control.

NMDA Receptor

N-methyl-D-aspartate receptor. Its activation causes central sensitization and is implicated in OIH.

OIH

Opioid-Induced Hyperalgesia - paradoxical increased pain sensitivity from chronic opioid exposure.
